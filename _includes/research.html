Task-Invariant Control and Design of Lower-Limb Exoskeletons
  • paper
We are using a novel control methodology for wearable robots that prefers task-invariant, energetic control rather than task-specific, kinematic control. It shapes the total energy of the human body with exoskeleton actuators, reducing mass/inertia parameters in body energetics to dynamically offload the weight of a patient. This methodology is ideally suited for task-invariant tasks because it does not depend on pre-defined kinematic patterns. Using powered orthoses to assist humans in a variety of activities is coming true.
Inertial Tail-like Appendage Use in Quadruped Improves Stability in Diagonal Sequence Walking Gaits
  • abstract
Our work aimed to increase the stability of the DS gait by investigating the use of an inertial tail-like appendage. We model the quadruped system as a self-manipulator with an actuated tail in 3D which can swing in not only the yaw direction but also the pitch direction.
Tail Use in Quadruped Improves Static Stability in Diagonal Sequence Walking Gaits
  • abstract
Our work investigates the use of a heavy tail to help stabilize larger-stride-displacement gaits for a quadruped robot. With the help of an actuated tail, such a quadruped can take advantage of the higher stride displacement (about 30% larger) of the diagonal sequence walking gait while maintaining static stability.

The gaits of quadrupedal animals all have footfall patterns by which contact is made between the foot and the ground. Both the order in which the feet make contact and the length of the contacts describe the gait. While there are a myriad of gaits quadrupeds could use for locomotion, only a few are routinely seen [hildebrand]. In this case, as Hildebrand diagram is a plot to characterize and compare symmetric quadruped gaits, we can perform gait design and stability analysis for quadruped, hexapod, octopod, etc.
Geometric Mechanics and Quadruped Back-bending
  • paper
  • video
  • slides
We used geometric mechanics to prescribe gaits with the coordination of leg movements and back-bending motion. We were able to show back-bending in quadruped, especially in salamanders, can improve stride displacement in the forward, rotational and lateral directions. Also, by assuming all stance feet having zero linear velocities, we modeled the quadruped locomotion on flat ground. We performed the simulation and robot test to analyze how back-bending improves locomotion.
